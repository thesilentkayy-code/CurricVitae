History of the Ruthven Clan

Historical and genealogical records do not indicate that Ada Agnes Ruthven's father, George Ruthven, made a specific formal request for his grandchildren to retain the Ruthven name. However, it was common practice in Victorian-era Britain for families to honor a mother's maiden name by using it as a middle name for their children, particularly when the mother's family had a notable history or social standing.

Yes, the Ruthven clan were part of the Scottish nobility. Their lineage and influence in Scottish history are directly tied to several noble titles. The family's noble status was cemented in 1488 when Sir William of Ruthven was created a Lord of Parliament, a hereditary peerage. This title, Lord Ruthven, gave them a seat in the Scottish Parliament and significant political power. In 1581, William Ruthven, the 4th Lord Ruthven, was further elevated to the title of Earl of Gowrie. This was a prestigious earldom, reflecting the family's influence as one of the most powerful Protestant houses in Scotland at the time.

However, the family's noble status was notoriously short-lived due to their repeated political intrigues against the monarchy. Following the Gowrie Conspiracy in 1600, King James VI, who believed the brothers John and Alexander Ruthven had attempted to kidnap or assassinate him, took dramatic action. The Scottish Parliament declared the Ruthven name and titles "extinct forever" and forfeited all their lands.

Despite the 17th-century proscription, the title of Earl of Gowrie was eventually revived in the 20th century. In 1945, a descendant of the clan, Major-General Sir Alexander Hore-Ruthven, was created Earl of Gowrie in recognition of his distinguished service, including his role as Governor-General of Australia. This act symbolically restored the family's standing and title, bringing a modern chapter to their long and turbulent history. History of the Murray Clan

The Murray clan is a prominent Scottish clan with a long and complex history, recognized as one of Scotland's most significant and enduring families for over 800 years. Its origins trace back to the 12th century and a Flemish knight named Freskin, who was granted lands in Moray, Scotland, by King David I. His descendants adopted the surname "de Moravia," meaning "from Moray," which eventually evolved into "Murray."

The clan's influence is also reflected in the Dukedom of Atholl, one of the most powerful titles in the Scottish peerage, whose holders have made significant contributions to both Scottish and British history.

My great-grandfather, Sir James Augustus Henry Murray, was a Scottish lexicographer and philologist who made a monumental contribution to the English language. He is best known as the primary editor of the Oxford English Dictionary (OED), a position he held from 1879 until his death in 1915. Born in Denholm, Scotland, Murray was a self-taught linguist who left school at age 14 and worked as a teacher for many years while pursuing his passion for philology.

When he took on the OED project in 1879, it was initially planned as a ten-year undertaking, but it grew into a massive work that defined his life's purpose. His innovative approach documented the historical evolution of words by tracing their origins and meanings through citations from different periods. To manage the immense volume of data, he built a special workroom called a "Scriptorium" and established a collaborative system that invited volunteers from around the world to submit quotations. His dedication and rigorous standards laid the foundation for the OED, which is now considered the definitive record of the English language. He was knighted in 1908 for his work.
